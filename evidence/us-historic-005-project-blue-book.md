Summary:
**THIS ACTUALLY HAPPENED.** The U.S. government ran an official UFO investigation for 17 years — and its real purpose might not have been finding truth.

**Project Blue Book** was the Air Force's public-facing UFO investigation program. On paper, it was supposed to determine if UFOs posed a national security threat and to scientifically analyze sightings.

In reality, many insiders say it was a **public relations operation** designed to make UFO witnesses look like idiots.

The program investigated **12,618 sightings**. Their conclusion? Most were misidentified aircraft, weather phenomena, or "psychological" cases (translation: "these people are crazy"). But here's the thing: **701 cases remained officially "unexplained"** even by their own analysis.

**The Robertson Panel (1953)**: A CIA-sponsored group recommended that the government should actively **debunk UFO sightings** to reduce public interest. Their explicit goal? Make the topic so embarrassing that serious people wouldn't touch it.

And boy, did it work.

**Simple analogy**: Imagine if the government created a program to study cancer, spent 17 years calling most cancer patients hypochondriacs, then announced "cancer isn't real, case closed." That's what Blue Book did to UFO research.

**Dr. J. Allen Hynek**, the astronomer who served as Blue Book's scientific consultant, later admitted he was pressured to debunk cases. He eventually became one of the most prominent UFO researchers and coined the term "Close Encounters."
