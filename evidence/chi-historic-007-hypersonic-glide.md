3. **Maneuvering reentry**: The vehicle changed course during descent — making tracking/interception nearly impossible
4. **Target accuracy**: Despite the complexity, it reportedly landed within 24 miles of its target (excellent for a first test)

General Mark Milley, Chairman of the Joint Chiefs, called it **"very concerning"** — which in military-speak means "we're freaking out."

**Why this doesn't add up:**

China's aerospace industry has historically lagged behind the U.S. and Russia. Their fighter jets use copied or licensed engines. Their space program, while impressive, built on Soviet foundations. But somehow hypersonic technology is where they **leap ahead**?

**The development timeline problem:**

- U.S. hypersonic research: Started 1960s, still not operational
- Russia hypersonic research: Started 1980s, recently achieved limited success
- China hypersonic research: Started ~2000s, operational orbital system in 2021

That's not how technological development normally works. Usually the country with the longest research history and biggest budget leads. China's budget is smaller, their start was later, yet they arrived first.

**Possible explanations:**

1. Chinese engineers are just better (possible, but unprecedented leap)
2. Massive espionage success (possible, but doesn't explain everything)
3. Access to technology from a non-traditional source (speculative but explanatory)

**Connection to other Chinese programs:**

- EmDrive research (China claims positive results while West abandoned it)
- Hypergravity research facility (studying extreme physics conditions)
- Trans-medium vehicle patents (suggesting broader exotic propulsion interest)

The pattern suggests China is either the greatest engineering culture in history, or they're benefiting from knowledge the rest of the world doesn't have access to.
